Origin

I started at UCF in 2016, I came in as a bio major with the "ambition" of being a physical therapist. In truth, I didn't know what I wanted to do, but my mom wasn't going to accept that as an answer haha. After two semesters I knew I didn't want to be a physical therapist. I hated all my classes, especially chemistry. I knew I needed to change directions, I googled, "degrees with good job availability." Of course CS was one of the top degrees. I had never programmed in my life, but I'm always open to new things. I watched some youtube videos and coded along, "hmm this isn't so bad" I thought. I was and am by no means a coding prodigy, but I worked hard to become proficient. I grew to really enjoy CS. The mathiness and rationale appeals to my nerd brain. After my very first programming class Intro to C, I began to help others develop their coding skills as well. I became a Supplemental Instruction(SI) Leader in 2017 at UCF. I held weekly sessions and helped others hone their coding craft. A year later and I became a more conventional tutor, also at UCF. Helping others learn has helped me create a very solid CS foundation for myself and introduced me to many great peers.

Tutoring also showed me my calling, I love to teach. There's no better feeling than helping someone learn. But before I can teach I want to be in the industry and gain some real-world experience. I want to be a developer. A project manager. A CTO. After my time in industry, I want to show others the magic that runs this whole planet, CS!

Pika Profession

Pika Profession

Developed for the IBM NA Intern 2020 Hackathon 4th/26

Group of 5
Front-end & Back-end

5 IBM NA interns grouped up for 2 days of caffiene-fueled mayhem. Developed in Bootstrap for frontend and Firebase for backend. Employers can register to post jobs and view resumes. Applicants register and can view jobs they're interested in. Then, they upload their resume and Watson AI blacks out their name anywhere on the resume. Once uploaded and blacked out, the employer takes a look at the resume and simply judges it off of the candidate's merit. If the employer is interested they can initiate a chat through a built in messaging service. Our names can be very telling of our gender, race, and ethnicity, those things shouldn't matter when you're hiring for a position.

I created the Firebase project and wrote a readme for everyone on the team to get on the same page, this allowed for everyone to work in parallel and test/integrate their work. I also worked on both the front-end and backend. I created the login and register pages and hooked it up to the Firebase authentication. I also hooked up the resume upload and storage. Users would upload their resume and it would get sent to Firebase Storage. Metadata was stored on who the resume belonged to and which jobs it had been applied to. There was a lot of learning on the fly for everyone so we weren't able to implement everything, but I'm very happy with how the result turned out.

Experience
IBM: Back End Intern

Worked on updating and maintaining Hyperledger Fabric.

Back End Intern

IBM

Durham, NC
June 15th - August 7th
The internship was completely virtual due to COVID. I was in the IBM Blockchain department, specifically working in Hyperledger Fabric. Fabric is an open source Blockchain platform used for secure and private transactions between different entities. It has many layers and moving pieces so the learning curve is fairly steep, fortunately everyone was very helpful and willing to share their knowledge with me.
I spent the majority of the internship working in Fabric-Samples. Samples is in the tutorial side of Fabric. Developers interested in learning about Fabric will start in Samples to play around with sample code. I developed sample smart contracts and applications for users to play around with and take a deeper look at. The samples are often used by companies as templates for their live apps and smart contracts. Smart Contracts are the business logic for making transactions in a Fabric network. I developed sample smart contracts in Java and JavaScript. For the Java contract, I used Gradle as a build tool, and used Junit and Mockito for unit testing. For both I used a test network for integration testing. After development, I would make a Github PR and have it checked out before getting merged into the main Fabric-Samples repo.

Applications are the API for users in a Fabric network. It is how a user can access the network and invoke their smart contract. I developed a couple sample applications in Java: app1 & app2. I became sort of the resident Java expert in my squad. I'm by no means an expert but that was the role I fell into and I took it in stride.
Overall it was an incredible experience. I learned so much in such a short period and got to meet so many amazing people. From the people in my squad, all the way up to the CTO of the department, everyone I spoke to was open and helpful. I spoke to countless IBMers that had been at IBM their whole career, or switched into IBM and never looked back. It all speaks volumes to the culture IBM has built within their company. I'm very grateful for my time there, and who knows, maybe I'll be an IBMer again down the road.
